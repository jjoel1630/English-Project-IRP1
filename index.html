About NASA

National Aeronautics and Space Administration

The National Aeronautics and Space Administration is America’s civil space program and the global leader in space exploration.

Core Values:

  • Curiosity: We are very curious and want to learn as much as we can.
  • Team-oriented: We solve problems and work as a team.
  • Excellence: We are always achieving excellence and we deliver our best products.
  • Agility: We are very flexible and we adjust to our environment regardless of the condition.
  • Resilience: We never give up, no matter how difficult of a situation we encounter.

NASA's Projects

Boeing B-29 Superfortress

One of the Most Technologically Advanced Airplanes of World War II

The B-29 had many features that were a part of the plane. One such feature that was added, allowed guns to be controlled remotely. The plane was the world’s heaviest production plane because it had to carry bombs, and the plane was also responsible for the bombings in Japan.

Learn More

Project Mercury

United States' First Man-in-Space Program

Project Mercury was the United States' first "man-in-space" program. We had a few goals which were to orbit the Earth with a manned spacecraft, learn about a human's capability to function in space, and to safely return the spacecraft from space to the Earth while leaving the pilot unharmed.

Learn More

Apollo 11

First Man on the Moon

In July of 1969, we launched the Apollo 11 mission. Our main objective was to send a human to the Moon safely. We also had additional goals which were to explore the moon by using the lunar module, deploy a television camera to transmit signals to Earth, deploy the solar wind composition experiment, the seismic experiment package, and the Laser Ranging Retroreflector.

Our Departments

Langley Aeronautical Laboratory

The Langley Aeronautical Laboratory rapidly expanded its capacity to test and improve new aircraft designs. Melvin Butler was the head of this department, and he was responsible for hiring new staff.

West Area Computing

The West Area Computing at the Langley Center was comprised of African-American female mathematicians, and were led by Margery Hannah. They also contributed to the World War II efforts.

East Area Computing

The East Area Computer at the Langley Center was comprised of White female mathematicians. They too contributed to the World War II efforts.

Flight Research Division

The Flight Research Division was established at Langley in 1917 to solve the difficulties and problems that existed in the aeronautics field. They ran tests and developed software to model the physics of aircraft. Wind tunnels and laboratories were also constructed to help test these new aircraft.

Pilotless Aircraft Research Division (PARD)

The Pilotless Aircraft Research Division was responsible for Langley’s space engineering development. Topics such as rocket propulsion, orbital physics, and atmospheric reentry were discussed, and solutions to these problems were created.

Space Task Group

The Space Task Group was responsible for the development of Project Mercury, whose objective was to send a man into the orbit of the Earth.
