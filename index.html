About NASA

National Aeronautics and Space Administration

We are National Aeronautics and Space Administration is America’s civil space program and the global leader in space exploration.

Core Values:

  • Curiosity: We’re continually asking questions and seeking answers
  • Team-oriented: We come together as one to solve complex issues.

NASA's Projects

Learn About the Projects which contributed to the creation of the National Aeronautics and Space Administration.

Boeing B-29 Superfortress

One of the Most Technologically Advanced Airplanes of World War II

The B-29 had many new features such as remote controlled guns. The plane was the world’s heaviest production plane because it had to carry bombs, and the plane was also responsible for the bombings in Japan.

Learn More

Project Mercury

United States' First Man-in-Space Program

Project Mercury was the United States' first man-in-space program. We had a few goals which were to orbit the Earth with a manned spacecraft, learn about a human's capability to function in space, and to safely return the spacecraft from space to the Earth while leaving the pilot unharmed.

Learn More

Apollo 11

First Man on the Moon

In July of 1969, the Moon-bound Apollo 11 mission launches. Our main objective was to send a human to the Moon safely. We also had additional goals which were to explore the moon by using the lunar module, deploy a television camera to transmit signals to Earth, and deploy the solar wind composition experiment, the seismic experiment package and the Laser Ranging Retroreflector.

Our Departments

Langley Aeronautical Laboratory

Langley Laboratory rapidly expanded its capacity to test and improve new aircraft designs. Melvin Butler was the head of personnel, and he was responsible for hiring new staff.

Learn More

West Computing

The West area at the Langley Center which comprised of African American female mathematicians and were led by Margery Hannah, and contributed to the World War II efforts.

Learn More

East Computing

The East area at the Langley Center which comprised of White female mathematicians and also contributed to the World War II efforts.

Learn More

Flight Research Division

The Flight Research Division was established at Langley in 1917 to solve the problems of flight such as run tests and developed software to model the physics of airflow on different aircraft. Wind tunnels and laboratories were constructed to help test these new aircraft.

Learn More

Pilotless Aircraft Research Division (PARD)

The Pilotless Aircraft Research Division was responsible for Langley’s space engineering expertise. Topics such as rocket propulsion, orbital physics, and atmospheric reentry were discussed, and solutions to these problems were created.

Learn More

Space Task Group

Responsible for the development of Project Mercury whose objective was to send a man into the orbit of space.
